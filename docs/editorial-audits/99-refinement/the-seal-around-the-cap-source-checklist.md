## Fact Checks

- FDA published the original tamper-resistant packaging final rule on November 5, 1982: PASS.
- The 1982 rule was prompted by malicious adulteration of OTC drug products and seven Chicago-area deaths described in the FDA preamble: PASS.
- The original rule covered most OTC human drug products, with exceptions and staggered effective dates: PASS.
- FDA defined tamper-resistant/tamper-evident logic around visible evidence to consumers if an indicator or barrier was breached or missing: PASS.
- FDA stated tamper-proof packaging was impossible and warned against tamper-proof labeling claims: PASS.
- Current 21 CFR 211.132 requires covered OTC drugs accessible to the public to use tamper-evident packaging: PASS.
- Current 21 CFR 211.132 requires labeling that identifies all tamper-evident features and remains unaffected when the feature is breached or missing: PASS.
- 1989 and later rulemaking records tightened the treatment of two-piece hard gelatin capsules: PASS.
- FDA's 1994 record considered but did not adopt a capsule ban, citing false-security concerns and capsule utility: PASS.
- FDA's 1998 final rule changed terminology to tamper-evident and required sealing technology for covered two-piece hard gelatin capsules: PASS.
- Public Law 98-127 created the Federal Anti-Tampering Act framework codified at 18 U.S.C. 1365: PASS.
- FDA enforcement material includes a warning-letter example involving the 21 CFR 211.132 labeling requirement: PASS.
- Accessibility and usability sources support cautious claims that medicine packaging can be hard for older adults and users with functional limits: PASS.
- Waste/material sources support cautious claims that blister packaging may involve composite materials that are difficult to separate and reuse: PASS.

## Caveats

- The essay uses the Tylenol crisis as the breach that exposed shelf-trust failure, not as an unsolved-crime narrative.
- It does not treat tamper-evident packaging as complete prevention.
- It does not treat consumer inspection as full safety assurance.
- It does not generalize European blister-waste estimates to the American OTC market.
- It uses FDA-cited industry cost estimates from rulemaking records, not independent audited industry cost data.
