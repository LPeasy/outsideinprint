# Whose Yes Source Checklist

## Source Hierarchy

1. Primary/official public data and public-health guidance.
2. Official context sources used only for cautious background.
3. Prior Research Library entries used only to avoid repetition and preserve source-role discipline.
4. Secondary reporting excluded from the governing frame.

## Primary, Official, Direct, Or Public-Record Sources Found

| Source | Type | Role |
|---|---|---|
| BLS, `American Time Use Survey Summary - 2025 A01 Results` | Official government data release | Supports the finite time-budget frame behind the discernment claim. |
| CDC, `FastStats: Sleep in Adults` | Official public-health guidance | Supports the adult seven-hour sleep recommendation as a boundary reminder, not as a causal claim. |
| CDC/NIOSH, `STRESS...At Work` and OSHA workplace stress page | Official context | Kept as background only; not used to make a causal burnout claim. |

## Secondary Reporting Used

None as governing evidence. Search results from secondary outlets were treated as leads only and not cited in the draft.

## Source Gaps

- No individual-level evidence supports a claim that one extra promise causes stress, insomnia, or family conflict.
- No source proves motive. The draft keeps approval-seeking language first-person, conditional, and conscience-based.
- No source proves a universal rule for every duty or emergency.

## Claims That Must Not Be Made As Certainty

- A fast yes always harms sleep, family, work, or rest.
- Overcommitment causes burnout in most cases.
- People who overcommit are acting in bad faith.
- Saying no is always wiser than saying yes.
- A fast yes always comes from vanity.

## Currentness Caveats

BLS ATUS and CDC sleep facts were live-checked on July 8, 2026.
