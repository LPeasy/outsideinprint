# Political Cartoon Design Philosophy

Outside In Print political cartoons make policy visible by turning it into a physical machine, trap, wound, or obstruction.

Each cartoon should take an abstract civic problem and convert it into one dominant, legible object-metaphor. The gallery language should be concrete enough that a reader understands the thesis before reading every label: a spreadsheet as an industrial smokestack, a shipping strait as a line of fire, corporate protection as a glass cloche, pesticide regulation as a stamped warning-label battlefield, or diplomacy as a phone cord chained to a blockade.

The house style is serious newspaper engraving with modern cinematic composition: black ink, dense crosshatching, heavy chiaroscuro, and a wide 16:9 stage. The subject should be styled in a classic American cartoon aesthetic. Backgrounds and darker elements should use modern high-contrast crosshatching.

The result should feel like a front-page civic argument rather than a joke. Use dramatic perspective, foregrounded props, and background silhouettes to create institutional scale without relying on recognizable politicians or slapstick caricature.

Color is used as argument, not decoration. Keep most of the image black, white, and gray. Use exactly one accent color to carry the moral or causal signal:

- green for spreadsheet logic, energy extraction, money flow, or institutional permission
- red for fire, fracture, crisis, harm, or rupture
- yellow for warning, hazard, caution, or regulatory notice
- blue for blocked channels, diplomacy, strategic waterways, or cold institutional distance

The accent color should guide the reader's eye through the logic of the cartoon rather than making the image feel fully colored.

Captions and labels are sparse but object-bound. Text belongs on signage, stamps, labels, screens, forms, locks, or charts inside the scene. The labels add specificity without turning the cartoon into an infographic.

## Review Standard

Before publishing or promoting a front-page cartoon, check that it has:

- one dominant physical metaphor for the policy problem
- a clear 16:9 composition with dramatic visual hierarchy
- serious black-ink newspaper engraving with dense crosshatching and heavy chiaroscuro
- one purposeful accent color, not a full-color palette
- sparse readable text physically attached to objects in the scene
- no recognizable politicians, real seals/logos, slapstick caricature, glossy rendering, or infographic clutter
