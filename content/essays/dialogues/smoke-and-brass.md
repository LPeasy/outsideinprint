*11 February 2026*

---

The room held its smoke the way a chapel holds incense ~ patiently, without complaint.

Brass lamps threw a tired amber across the tables. Glasses sweated. Somewhere behind the bar a piano worked through a standard that had lost its name but not its shape. The night had slowed to a crawl, which was how Oliver liked it.

Syd sat across from him, jacket off, sleeves rolled, cigarette resting untouched between his fingers. He always forgot to smoke when he was thinking. Oliver had noticed that years ago and never mentioned it.

They had been talking about nothing important ~ a book Syd had abandoned halfway through, a woman Oliver once loved and never quite stopped, the peculiar way cities repeat themselves across centuries. The easy stuff. The shared air of men who trust each other enough not to perform.

Oliver turned his glass once, watching the light bend, not looking up.

**Oliver:** Can I ask you something?

Syd smiled faintly.

**Syd:** You just did.

Oliver breathed out through his nose. He was choosing his words carefully, not because he feared offense, but because he didn’t yet understand what he was confessing.

**Oliver:** I don’t know how anyone denies that objective reality exists. I mean that sincerely. I can’t map it. I’ve tried.

Syd didn’t answer immediately. He set the cigarette down, unlit. The pause was not strategic ~ it was honest.

**Syd:** You want the real answer.

**Oliver:** Yes.

**Syd:** I don’t think most people deny it. I think they retreat from it.

Oliver frowned, just slightly.

**Oliver:** That sounds like wordplay.

**Syd:** It isn’t.

Syd leaned back.

**Syd:** It’s motive.

Oliver waited. He always did.

Syd gestured loosely toward the window, where the night pressed up against the glass like a fact that didn’t care to be interpreted.

**Syd:** You can say life is a simulation. A projection. A story. A construct. People love those words. They sound sophisticated. But simulations are models. Models of something. Lenses don’t exist without light. Maps don’t exist without territory.

**Oliver:** So why pretend otherwise?

Syd looked at him now, fully.

**Syd:** Because objective reality means you can be wrong.

Oliver felt something tighten ~ not disagreement, but recognition.

**Syd:** It means your inner world isn’t sovereign. It means resentment doesn’t rewrite the structure of things. Trauma doesn’t get veto power. Preference doesn’t become law.

He paused.

**Oliver:** For someone who’s been humiliated by the world ~ really humiliated ~ that can feel unbearable. So they deny the world instead.

**Syd:** They deny accountability to it.

Syd corrected.

**Syd:** There’s a difference.

Oliver took a drink. The whiskey burned clean.

**Oliver:** You can say the sky isn’t blue. You can insist it’s green. Or purple. But if you walk outside, it’s still blue. You have to put on yellow glasses to make it green.

Syd nodded.

**Syd:** And once you’re wearing them, anyone who insists the sky is blue becomes the liar.

**Oliver:** Worse. The oppressor.

**Syd:** Yes.

Oliver shook his head.

**Oliver:** And then you tell me I’m wearing glasses. Invisible ones. Ones I can’t remove.

Syd smiled, but there was no humor in it.

**Syd:** That’s the trick. A lens that only exists to explain away contradiction.

Oliver leaned forward, elbows on the table.

**Oliver:** For what possible reason, would I accept the premise that the sky is green?

Syd met his eyes.

**Syd:** You wouldn’t. Unless the alternative felt worse.

The piano stumbled and recovered. A waiter passed, collected an empty glass, moved on.

Oliver stared at the table for a moment, then looked back up.

**Oliver:** Do you believe objective reality exists?

It was not a challenge. It was not a joke. It was the kind of question you ask only when you are prepared to hear an answer you don’t want.

Syd didn’t hesitate.

**Syd:** No. Not in the way you mean.

The words landed without force. That was what frightened Oliver ~ not their sharpness, but their calm.

**Oliver:** You don’t believe there are true statements about the world.

**Syd:** I believe there are experiences. Interpretations. Frameworks.

**Oliver:** And constraints?

Syd considered.

**Syd:** Constraints that matter only insofar as we agree they do.

Oliver felt the room tilt, just a fraction.

**Oliver:** That’s madness.

He stopped himself and softened his voice.

**Oliver:** I don’t mean that as an insult. I mean I don’t see how language survives that. Or history. Or morality.

Syd folded his hands.

**Syd:** Morality survives just fine. It just stops pretending to be universal.

Oliver exhaled slowly.

**Oliver:** Which of these is better, walking an old woman across the street ~ or kicking a baby in the head?

Syd didn’t answer immediately. His jaw tightened.

**Syd:** That’s not fair.

**Oliver:** It’s the cleanest case. Everyone knows the answer. Across cultures. Across centuries. By recognition.

Syd looked away.

**Oliver:** That recognition tracks something real. Vulnerability. Harm. Asymmetry. Responsibility. How is that possible if there’s no objective truth?

Syd turned back.

**Syd:** Because we’re the same kind of animal. Embodied. Social. Fragile.

**Oliver:** And that’s real.

**Syd:** It’s experienced.

Oliver sat back. Something in him had already begun to grieve, though he didn’t yet know why.

**Oliver:** If there’s no objective truth, then there’s no true history. No accurate account. No moral authority beyond feeling.

**Syd:** There’s accountability. To each other.

**Oliver:** That collapses into power. Eventually.

Syd didn’t deny it.

They sat in the smoke, the space between them suddenly wider than the table.

After a while, Oliver spoke again.

**Oliver:** I don’t think I can fully trust someone who doesn’t believe truth exists outside their own mind.

He said it the way one says a diagnosis ~ not angry, not triumphant. Sad.

Syd nodded.

**Syd:** You shouldn’t.

The piano reached the end of the tune and didn’t start another.

Syd stood, slipped his jacket on, already somewhere else.

**Syd:** Good talk.

Syd meant it.

Oliver watched him go, the words echoing long after Syd had forgotten them.
