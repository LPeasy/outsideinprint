For decades, U.S. presidents have relied on executive agencies to carry out policy agendas, often stretching the boundaries of statutory interpretation to fit their priorities. However, agencies have never been seamless instruments of executive will. Bureaucratic inertia, statutory constraints, and internal resistance have long complicated their responsiveness.

The Supreme Court's ruling in Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo (2024) overturned Chevron deference, shifting the center of power over statutory interpretation from executive agencies to the judiciary. Agencies have always faced legal and political hurdles in executing presidential priorities, but this ruling replaces Chevron-based flexibility with a new layer of judicial scrutiny, further slowing an already complex process.

As the federal government seeks to improve efficiency and responsiveness, this ruling presents a paradox: Can agencies function as reliable executors of executive will and congressional intent, or has judicial oversight made responsiveness even more difficult?

## The Constitutional Basis of Federal Bureaucracy and the Role of the Judiciary

The U.S. Constitution does not explicitly create federal agencies. Instead, it grants Congress the authority to establish departments and define their powers under Article I, Section 8, which provides that Congress may "make all Laws which shall be necessary and proper for carrying into Execution" its enumerated powers. This clause laid the groundwork for the creation of executive departments, beginning with the Department of State, Treasury, and War in 1789.

The president's authority over these agencies stems from Article II, Section 1, which vests "the executive power" in the president, and Article II, Section 3, which requires the president to "take Care that the Laws be faithfully executed." However, these clauses have historically been interpreted to mean that agencies serve a dual role: they execute presidential directives but do so within the framework of laws enacted by Congress.

The Chevron doctrine was grounded in the assumption that when Congress wrote ambiguous statutes, it implicitly delegated interpretive authority to the agencies enforcing them. This doctrine allowed agencies to craft policies without requiring explicit legislative clarity as long as their interpretations were "reasonable." However, in Loper Bright, the Supreme Court rejected this assumption, stating that ambiguity in a statute does not constitute congressional delegation. Instead, the Court reaffirmed that under the Administrative Procedure Act (APA), Section 706, it is the role of courts, not agencies, to interpret statutory meaning.

This shift reconfigures agency authority. Agencies have always had to weigh executive intent against legal constraints, but now, every significant policy decision will face heightened judicial review, replacing administrative discretion with stricter legal scrutiny.

## Why Was Chevron Deference Created?

The Chevron doctrine was built on three core justifications: agency expertise, political accountability, and administrative efficiency. Courts deferred to agencies because they had technical expertise and operated within the executive branch, making them politically accountable to elected officials. Chevron also streamlined governance, allowing agencies to fill legislative gaps without prolonged judicial intervention.

However, this legal landscape gradually shifted. The Supreme Court narrowed Chevron's scope over time, excluding informal agency guidance from receiving deference and imposing stricter requirements for rulemaking. Loper Bright was not an abrupt reversal but rather the culmination of decades of judicial skepticism toward unelected bureaucrats wielding expansive authority. The Court ultimately ruled that deference gave agencies too much power over statutory interpretation, contradicting the requirement that courts, not agencies, exercise independent judgment in legal interpretation.

With Chevron overturned, the balance of power in administrative law fundamentally shifted.

## The End of Chevron vs. the Push for Agency Responsiveness

With Chevron overturned, agencies no longer enjoy automatic deference in interpreting ambiguous statutes. However, courts may still apply Skidmore deference, a weaker form of deference in which agency interpretations are considered persuasive but not binding. This means that agencies must now justify their interpretations not just to the executive branch but also to the courts, creating an additional layer of legal scrutiny.

The end of Chevron fundamentally changes how agencies respond to executive directives. Agencies have long struggled to balance executive priorities with statutory interpretation, bureaucratic inertia, and shifting political directives. Now, rather than navigating ambiguity with Chevron-enabled flexibility, they must anticipate heightened judicial scrutiny, further slowing an already complex process.

This new reality creates a paradox:

1. Agencies may become less responsive, not because of internal resistance, but because judicial scrutiny now constrains executive directives more than bureaucratic inertia ever did.

2. Executive orders alone may not be enough to drive policy shifts. Agencies will now need stronger statutory justification before acting.

For instance, challenges to EPA vehicle emissions rules show how agencies may have to defend statutory authority more directly than they did under Chevron. Loper Bright forces agencies to anticipate judicial pushback, which can slow their ability to act on executive priorities.

In essence, Loper Bright may not create new inefficiencies so much as it replaces one form of administrative discretion with another layer of legal caution.

## Conclusion: The Shift in Power and the Future of Agency Responsiveness

The Supreme Court's decision in Loper Bright is not just a procedural shift. It is a fundamental redistribution of power between the executive branch, the judiciary, and the administrative state. By eliminating Chevron, the Court has redefined agency discretion, making bureaucratic responsiveness to executive intent dependent on explicit congressional authorization and judicial approval.

This shift fundamentally alters how agencies navigate legal ambiguity. Agencies have never been perfectly responsive to executive will, as statutory limitations and bureaucratic inertia have always shaped their actions. But rather than using Chevron-backed discretion to interpret ambiguous laws, they now face a new constraint: the looming presence of judicial intervention. The elimination of Chevron may not create new inefficiencies so much as it replaces administrative discretion with heightened judicial oversight.

In this new legal landscape, the machinery of governance has not just slowed down. It has been structurally reengineered. If executive intent is now subordinate to judicial oversight, the administrative state is no longer an extension of presidential power. It is an institution shaped by the courts.
